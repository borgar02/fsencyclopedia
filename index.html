Demihuman (not to be confused with demigod)


Demihumans (alternate: halfling) [demi-(half, partial) + human], like their name suggests, are a humanoid race that consists of many subraces. Their human features are usually combined with an animal's, however some demihumans are depicted with insect wings (see fairies), antennae, exo-skeletons, or even spider legs. Most common animal traits of demi-humans are animal ears and a tail.

Amphithere


Amphithere by Edward Topsell(1608)

An Amphithere (alternate: Amphitere, Phipthere)[meaning bi-winged, two-winged] is a type of winged serpent found in European heraldry.

Amphipteres generally were said to have greenish-yellow feathers, a serpentine body similar to a lindwurm, bat-like green wings with feathered bone, and an arrow-tipped tail much like a wyvern's. Others are described as entirely covered in feathers with a spiked tail, bird-like wings, and a beak-like snout. Even more uncommon is the description of one with legs.



Cockatrice


A cockatrice overdoor at Belvedere Castle (1869) in New York's Central Park

A cockatrice [Old French: cocatris, Latin: calcatrix] is a mythical beast, essentially a two-legged dragon or serpent-like creature with a rooster's head.

According to Alexander Neckam's De naturis rerum (cca 1180), the cockatrice was the product of an egg laid by a rooster and incubated by a toad; a snake might be substituted in re-tellings. Cockatrice became seen as synonymous with basilisk when the basiliscus in Bartholomeus Anglicus' De proprietatibus rerum (cca 1260) was translated by John Trevisa as cockatrice (1397). A basilisk, however, is usually depicted without wings.

It has the reputed ability to kill people by either looking at them — "the death-darting eye of Cockatrice" — touching them, or sometimes breathing on them.



Dragon


A dragon is a large, serpentine legendary creature that appears in the folklore of many cultures around the world. Beliefs about dragons vary drastically by region, but dragons in western cultures since the High Middle Ages have often been depicted as winged, horned, four-legged, and capable of breathing fire. Dragons in eastern cultures are usually depicted as wingless, four-legged, serpentine creatures with above-average intelligence.

Illustration of a winged, fire-breathing dragon by Friedrich Justin Bertuch from 1806

The popular western image of a dragon is based on a conflation of earlier dragons from different traditions, and of inaccurate scribal drawings of snakes. In western cultures, dragons are portrayed as monsters to be tamed or overcome, usually by saints or culture heroes.



Drake


Drake is often used as a synonym for dragon, however they can have different meanings. A drake is sometimes reffered to as a smaller dragon. It also sometimes has no wings on its back.


Fae


Fae (alternate: faerie dragon) is a small breed of dragons, usually as big as a medium-sized dog. They are sometimes found in modern fantasy works as wizards' familiars or pets, along with salamanders.



Hydra


Gustave Moreau's 19th-century depiction of the Hydra, influenced by the Beast from the Book of Revelation.

The Hydra (alternate: Lernaean Hydra, Hydra of Lerna) is a serpentine water monster in Greek and Roman mythology. Lerna was reputed to be an entrance to the Underworld. In the canonical Hydra myth, the monster is killed by Heracles (Hercules) as the second of his Twelve Labors.

It is often described as a multi-headed dragon-like creature with no wings that lures in a swamp. Its special property is that if you cut off a head, two more will regrow.



Kirin


The Kirin (alternate: girin, qilin)[kirin also happens to mean giraffe in japanese] is a mythical hooved chimerical creature known in Chinese and other East Asian cultures, said to appear with the imminent arrival or passing of a sage or illustrious ruler. Kirin is a specific type of the lin mythological family of one-horned beasts.

Kirin generally have Chinese dragon-like features. Most notably their heads, eyes with thick eyelashes, manes that always flow upward and beards. The body is fully or partially scaled and often shaped like an ox, deer, or horse. They are always shown with cloven hooves. In modern times, the depictions of kirins have often fused with the Western concept of unicorns.



Lindwurm


Lindworm in the arms of the small Bavarian town of Wurmannsquick

A Lindwurm (alternate: Lindworm, Lindorm, Linnorm)[Old Norse: linnormr (ensnaring snake)] is either a legendary dragon-like creature or serpent monster. In British heraldry, lindworm is a technical term for a wingless serpentine monster with two clawed arms in the upper body. In Norwegian heraldry a lindorm is the same as the wyvern in British heraldry.

The most common depiction of lindworm is a wingless creature with a serpentine body, a dragon-like head, scaled or reptilian skin and two clawed arms in the upper body. The most common depiction of them implies that such lindworms do not walk on their two limbs like a wyvern, but move like a mole lizard: they slither like a snake but they also use their arms to move themselves.



Lung Dragon


Chinese / East Asian dragon

The Lung Dragon (alternate: Chinese dragon, Long, Lung) is a legendary creature in Chinese mythology, Chinese folklore, and East Asian culture at large. Lung Dragons have many animal-like forms such as turtles and fish, but are most commonly depicted as snake-like with four legs. They symbolize potent and auspicious powers, particularly control over water, rainfall, typhoons and floods. It is also a symbol of strength, power and good luck (in East Asian culture).

Of the 117 scales, 81 are of the yang essence (positive) while 36 are of the yin essence (negative).

One famous Lung Dragon is Shenlong (lit. god dragon), a spiritual dragon from Chinese mythology who is the master of storms and also a bringer of rain. He is of equal significance to other creatures such as Tianlong, the celestial dragon. It is also depicted in the manga series Dragon Ball as a wish-granting dragon that kicks up a storm once summoned.



Quetzalcoatl


Quetzalcoatl in feathered-serpent form as depicted in the Codex Telleriano-Remensis

Quetzalcoatl is the Aztec god of wind, air, and learning. He is often depicted as a shape-shifting god, having a somewhat humanoid form and a feathered-serpent form (especially in Mesoamerican history).

To the Aztecs, Quetzalcoatl was, as his name indicates, a feathered serpent, a flying reptile (much like a dragon), who was a boundary-maker (and transgressor) between earth and sky. He was a creator deity having contributed essentially to the creation of Mankind.



Salamander


The Salamander is an amphibian of the order Urodela which, as with many real creatures, often has been ascribed fantastic and sometimes occult qualities (not possesed by the real organism) by pre-modern authors. The legendary salamander is often depicted as a typical salamander in shape, with a lizard-like form, but is usually ascribed an affinity with fire, sometimes specifically elemental fire.

Salamanders are often depicted in fictional works as a fire-breathing dragon with no wings, red scales, and three red feathers on each side of the creature's head.



Sea Serpent


The Destruction of Leviathan by Gustave Dore (1865)

A Sea Serpent (alternate: Sea Dragon) is a type of dragon described in various mythologies, must notably Greek, Mesopotamian, Hebrew, and Norse.

In antiquity and in the Bible, dragons were envisioned as huge serpentine monsters, which means that the image of a dragon with two or four legs and wings came much later during the Late Middle Ages. Stories depicting sea-dwelling serpents may include the Babylonian myths of Tiamat, the myths of the Hydra, Scylla, Cetus and Echidna in the Greek mythology, and even the Leviathan.



Wyrm


The Wyrm is a smaller type of sea-serpent that can also be found on land. It is said to bring luck to the people who find one.

In Pokemon, this monster appears in 3 evolutionary forms: Dratini, Dragonair (both having similarities to sea serpents) and Dragonite (a bipedal orange dragon). This pokemon is rare, since its final evolution is powerful.



Wyvern


A Wyvern (alternate: wivern) is a legendary bipedal dragon with a tail often ending in a diamond- or arrow-shaped tip. The wyvern in heraldry and folklore is rarely fire-breathing like the four-legged dragon. Sometimes modern fantasy book and media authors portray wyverns that are frost-breathing or poison-breathing instead of fire-breathing.

Dwarf


Dwarves as depicted in the trilogy Lord Of The Rings

In Germanic mythology, a dwarf [Old English: dweorg, pl. dwarfs / dwarves] is a human-shaped entity that dwells in mountains and in the earth, and is variously associated with wisdom, smithing, mining, and crafting. Dwarfs usually have advanced infrastructure, especially for a fantasy setting. Dwarfs are usually depicted with beards.

Many dwarfs have the ability to make themselves invisible, typically via a "Tarnkappe" (cloak of invisibility), which seems to be a very old attribute of the dwarfs. They also possess other magical objects, and often appear as master smiths.

Typically they live inside of hollow mountains, though in some cases they may live above the ground. Depending on the story, they may be hostile or friendly to humans. Male dwarfs are often portrayed as lusting after human women, whereas female dwarfs seek to possess the male hero in the legends.

Fairy


Depiction of a fairy by an artist (found on pinterest)

A fairy [Old French, faie: woman skilled in magic] is a type of mythical being or legendary creature in European folklore (and particularly Celtic, Slavic, German, English, and French folklore), a form of spirit, often described as metaphysical. The label of fairy applies to specific magical creatures with human appearence, small stature and magical powers, however it was once used as a name to describe any magical creature, such as goblins or gnomes. It can also be used as an adjective to describe something as being "enchanted" or "magical".

Fairies usually have the appearence of a small female human with butterfly or dragonfly wings on its back, however they have also been depicted as hummingbirds (see Rise of the Guardians' Toothiana). Fairies are also sometimes thought to haunt specific locations, and to lead travelers astray using will-o'-the-wisps.

Wings, while common in Victorian and later artworks, are rare in folklore; fairies flew by means of magic, sometimes perched on ragwort stems or the backs of birds.

Giant


The giants Fafner and Fasolt seize Freyja in Arthur Rackham's illustration of Richard Wagner's Der Ring des Nibelungen.

Giants (alternate: titan) [from Latin and Ancient Greek: gigas, derived from the Gigantes of Greek mythology] are humanoid beings that usually are prodigious in size and strength.

Representing human body enlarged to the point of being monstrous, giants evoke terror and remind humans of their body's frailty and mortality. They are often portrayed as monsters and antagonists, but there are exceptions. Some giants intermingle with human in friendly way, and can even be part of human families, with their offspring being portrayed as regular humans.

Gnome


Gnome with newspaper and tobacco pipe, by Heinrich Schlitt (1923)

Gnomes [latin: gnomus] are typically said to be small humanoid creatures that live underground. In some fairytales, the term gnome becomes synonymous with the term goblin (see Goblin), however modern fiction depicts them as being totally different creatures.

Gnomes are typically depicted in modern fantasy in a cunning role, as inventors. J. R. R. Tolkien's depicts gnomes as being the most gifted and technologically minded of his elvish (see elf) races. His "Gnomes" are generally tall, beautiful, dark-haired, light-skinned, immortal, and typically wise but suffer from pride, tend towards violence, and have an overweening love of the works of their own hands, particularly gemstones. Many of them live in cities below ground or in secluded mountain fortresses.

Immortality is a common gnome trait in fantasy literature.

Gnomes are sometimes confused with dwarfs, because of their similarities.

Golem


In Jewish folklore, a golem is an animated anthropomorphic being that is created entirely from inanimate material (usually clay or mud, but also rocks).

Golems are not intelligent, and if commanded to perform a task, they will perform the instructions literally. In many depictions, Golems are inherently perfectly obedient.

In some depictions, golems can be assembled and disassembled, being comprised of many parts. Using magic, these can be given life and even personalities of their own - at the will of their creator.

In other works of fiction, golems are independent beings that do not exist as creations of other races.

Merfolk


Merfolk by Jason Engle (male)

Mermaid (female)

Merfolk (alternate: merpeople, fishpeople, fishmen) are legendary water-dwelling human-like species. They are attested in folklore and mythology throughout the ages in various parts of the world.

The female merfolk may be referred to as mermaids, although in a strict sense mermaids are confined to beings who are half-woman and half-fish in appearance. Male aquatic human-like beings are called mermen.



Females (mermaids)


John William Waterhouse, "A Mermaid" (1900)

"Ningyo no zu"

In folklore, a mermaid [mere (sea) + maid (maiden), Old English: merwif] is an aquatic creature with the head and upper body of a female human and the tail of a fish. Mermaids are sometimes associated with perilous events such as floods, storms, shipwrecks, and drownings.

The conception of mermaids in the West may have been influenced by the Sirens of Greek mythology, which were originally half-birdlike, but came to be pictured as half-fishlike in the Christian era. Historical accounts of mermaids, such as those reported by Christopher Columbus during his exploration of the Caribbean, may have been sightings of manatees, dugongs or similar aquatic mammals. While there is no evidence that mermaids exist outside folklore, reports of mermaid sightings continue to the present day.

In some works of fiction, mermaids are simply portrayed as identical to humans, but with the ability to breathe and live underwater.

In some other works of fiction, mermaids are said to be able to split their tails once they reach a certain age, therefore being able to walk on land like regular humans.

Slavic counterpart: Rusalkas (also similiar to sirens and naiads, often portrayed as mailicious toward mankind)

Japanese counterpart: ningyo = a being that is usually part human female and part fish. This creature is sometimes depicted without a human torso, and can also be considered a yōkai.

Males (mermen)


Mermen (alternate: triton, fishman, sea-satyr{for the land version, see satyr}, sea-devil), the counterparts of the mythical female mermaids, are legendary creatures which are male human from the waist up and fish-like from the waist down, but may assume normal human shape. Sometimes described as hideous, and other times as handsome. A "merboy" is a young merman.

The actions and behavior of mermen can vary wildly depending on the source and time period of the stories. They have been said to sink ships by summoning great storms, but also said to be wise teachers, according to earlier mythology. Mermen, just like mermaids, can lure and attract humans with their enchantingly beautiful, soft melodic and seductive siren-like singing voices and tones.

In Medieval Europe, mermen were sometimes held responsible for causing violent storms and sinking ships.

Mermen are sometimes very different from their female counterparts, having more prevailing fish features, such as scales on their torso, limbs and heads, fins on their heads and gills on their necks.

Demihuman (not to be confused with demigod)


Demihumans (alternate: halfling) [demi-(half, partial) + human], like their name suggests, are a humanoid race that consists of many subraces. Their human features are usually combined with an animal's, however some demihumans are depicted with insect wings (see fairies), antennae, exo-skeletons, or even spider legs. Most common animal traits of demi-humans are animal ears and a tail.

Dwarf


Dwarves as depicted in the trilogy Lord Of The Rings

In Germanic mythology, a dwarf [Old English: dweorg, pl. dwarfs / dwarves] is a human-shaped entity that dwells in mountains and in the earth, and is variously associated with wisdom, smithing, mining, and crafting. Dwarfs usually have advanced infrastructure, especially for a fantasy setting. Dwarfs are usually depicted with beards.

Many dwarfs have the ability to make themselves invisible, typically via a "Tarnkappe" (cloak of invisibility), which seems to be a very old attribute of the dwarfs. They also possess other magical objects, and often appear as master smiths.

Typically they live inside of hollow mountains, though in some cases they may live above the ground. Depending on the story, they may be hostile or friendly to humans. Male dwarfs are often portrayed as lusting after human women, whereas female dwarfs seek to possess the male hero in the legends.

Fairy


Depiction of a fairy by an artist (found on pinterest)

A fairy [Old French, faie: woman skilled in magic] is a type of mythical being or legendary creature in European folklore (and particularly Celtic, Slavic, German, English, and French folklore), a form of spirit, often described as metaphysical. The label of fairy applies to specific magical creatures with human appearence, small stature and magical powers, however it was once used as a name to describe any magical creature, such as goblins or gnomes. It can also be used as an adjective to describe something as being "enchanted" or "magical".

Fairies usually have the appearence of a small female human with butterfly or dragonfly wings on its back, however they have also been depicted as hummingbirds (see Rise of the Guardians' Toothiana). Fairies are also sometimes thought to haunt specific locations, and to lead travelers astray using will-o'-the-wisps.

Wings, while common in Victorian and later artworks, are rare in folklore; fairies flew by means of magic, sometimes perched on ragwort stems or the backs of birds.

Giant


The giants Fafner and Fasolt seize Freyja in Arthur Rackham's illustration of Richard Wagner's Der Ring des Nibelungen.

Giants (alternate: titan) [from Latin and Ancient Greek: gigas, derived from the Gigantes of Greek mythology] are humanoid beings that usually are prodigious in size and strength.

Representing human body enlarged to the point of being monstrous, giants evoke terror and remind humans of their body's frailty and mortality. They are often portrayed as monsters and antagonists, but there are exceptions. Some giants intermingle with human in friendly way, and can even be part of human families, with their offspring being portrayed as regular humans.

Gnome


Gnome with newspaper and tobacco pipe, by Heinrich Schlitt (1923)

Gnomes [latin: gnomus] are typically said to be small humanoid creatures that live underground. In some fairytales, the term gnome becomes synonymous with the term goblin (see Goblin), however modern fiction depicts them as being totally different creatures.

Gnomes are typically depicted in modern fantasy in a cunning role, as inventors. J. R. R. Tolkien's depicts gnomes as being the most gifted and technologically minded of his elvish (see elf) races. His "Gnomes" are generally tall, beautiful, dark-haired, light-skinned, immortal, and typically wise but suffer from pride, tend towards violence, and have an overweening love of the works of their own hands, particularly gemstones. Many of them live in cities below ground or in secluded mountain fortresses.

Immortality is a common gnome trait in fantasy literature.

Gnomes are sometimes confused with dwarfs, because of their similarities.
